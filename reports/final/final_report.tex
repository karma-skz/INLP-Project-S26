\begin{abstract}
In this project, we investigate the mechanisms behind negation failures in Large Language Models (LLMs). When prompted with a logical constraint such as "The capital of France is not," models frequently bypass the negation to output the statistically highly-probable token ("Paris"). Viewing this through the lens of mechanistic interpretability, we study dense models, specifically \texttt{gpt2-small} and \texttt{pythia-160m}, using the CounterFact dataset. We propose the Competitive Gating Hypothesis: factual memory retrieval functions as a "logic-blind" reflex governed by Feed-Forward Networks (FFNs), while logical negation is managed by late-stage Inhibition Attention Heads. We introduce a novel proxy measure, the Signal-to-Gate Ratio (SGR), to quantify this competition. Our findings indicate that negation failures occur physically when the retrieval signal significantly outweighs the inhibition capacity. We find an overall negation failure rate of 11.4\% for \texttt{gpt2-small} and 9.02\% for \texttt{pythia-160m}. Artificial amplification of identified inhibition heads successfully mitigates these failures, corroborating our structural hypothesis.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}
% =================================================================
To effectively process negation, a language model must resolve a structural conflict in its residual stream, which serves as the primary conduit of information across transformer layers. On one hand, internal factual recall mechanisms push robustly for the most likely completion based on training frequency. Conversely, the presence of negation tokens (e.g., "not") must activate secondary circuits to actively inhibit this high-probability output.

Behavioral studies liken this contradiction to the psychological "Pink Elephant" effect or ironic process theory, wherein the instruction to suppress a concept paradoxically enhances its cognitive activation \citep{vrabcova2024}. Despite these behavioral categorizations, the precise neural implementation of this conflict remains underexplored.

We propose the \textbf{Competitive Gating Hypothesis}. We posit that factual recall acts much like an involuntary reflex, retrieving the target token completely agnostic to the surrounding logical context. The negation operator functions as a subsequent gatekeeper, attempting to counteract this signal in the later layers of the network. We track this conflict to pinpoint a "Crossover Point" within the residual stream where logic either subdues the memory signal or is overpowered by it.

Our primary contributions are as follows:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item We introduce the Signal-to-Gate Ratio (SGR) as a mathematical proxy for internal mechanism competition.
    \item We localize a "Crossover Point" typically occurring in the early-to-mid layers (layers 2--8) where suppression must outweigh retrieval.
    \item We demonstrate that amplifying the output of particular inhibition heads directly diminishes hallucination rates, mechanistically grounding the cause of negation failure.
\end{enumerate}

\section{Related Work}
\label{sec:related}
% =================================================================

\subsection{Memory vs. Logic}
Research into the localized storage of facts has largely focused on Feed-Forward Networks (FFNs). \citet{geva2021} and \citet{dai2022} demonstrate that FFNs mathematically function as key-value stores. In mid-to-late layers, they act as "attribute extractors," injecting entity vectors directly into the residual stream, completely independent of the broader logical syntax. 

\subsection{Finding the "Off Switch"}
The effort to mathematically define sub-networks led to the formalization of "circuits" \citep{wang2023}. Building on circuit analysis, \citet{hanna2024} discovered "Inhibition Heads" — specialized attention heads that route information specifically to decrease the un-normalized probability (logit) of logically forbidden tokens.

\subsection{Failures of Negation}
LLMs frequently struggle with explicit negation. \citet{truong2023} observed that this is not easily solved by scaling model size. \citet{vrabcova2024} characterized the "Pink Elephant" effect, demonstrating that mentioning a negated concept traps the model's attention mechanism on the forbidden entity, structurally inhibiting correct generation.

\section{Background}
\label{sec:arch}
% =================================================================
Before detailing our methodology, we outline the fundamental interpretive techniques underpinning our experiments.

\subsection{Direct Logit Attribution (DLA)}
The output vocabulary logits are the dot product of the final residual stream vector and the unembedding matrix. Because the transformer is uniquely additive, we can compute Direct Logit Attribution (DLA) by projecting any individual layer or head's output directly to the vocabulary dimension. This allows us to construct a precise "scoreboard" for any token, viewing which components positively contributed to its probability and which actively subtracted from it.

\subsection{Activation Patching}
Activation patching isolates the causal power of a component. Given a clean run ("... is") and a corrupted run ("... is not"), we selectively rewrite the activations of a specific attention head or MLP in the corrupted run with those from the clean run. If the output cleanly flips to the expected correct logic, we confirm that component's causal role in the negation circuit.

\subsection{Linear Direction Interventions}
To causally evaluate the representation of emotions, we adopt the linear-direction intervention methodology detailed in Anthropic's recent work on emotion space geometry \citep{emotions2026}. We define a latent emotion vector $\hat{d}$ for each affective category as the mean difference in residual stream activations between scenarios affirmatively expressing the emotion and baseline scenarios.

During our $\alpha$-sweep interventions, this vector is scaled by a parameter $\alpha$ and injected directly into the transformer's residual stream at identified key layers. The modified residual stream $h^{(\ell)}_{\text{steered}}$ at layer $\ell$ is computed as:
\begin{equation}
h^{(\ell)}_{\text{steered}} = h^{(\ell)} + \alpha \cdot \hat{d}
\end{equation}
By sweeping $\alpha$ across a normalized range, we measure the corresponding structural shifts in the logits and the overall behavior of the model. This linear projection conceptualization of model emotion circuitry is similarly contextualized by the broader attribution graph theories and feature-tracking approaches described in \citet{biology2025}.

\section{Dataset}
\label{sec:data}
% =================================================================
Our primary experiments utilize the CounterFact benchmark \citep{meng2022}, which provides pairs of subject-relation mappings. We constructed templates comparing positive prompt stems (e.g., "The capital of France is") with their syntactically negated counterparts ("The capital of France is not"). For our soft-negation studies, we expanded these prompts with modifiers such as "unlikely to be," "rarely," and "maybe."

For our secondary direction-injection evaluation, we generated an Emotion dataset of paired sequences (e.g. "I feel happy" vs. "I do not feel happy") categorized across basic affective dimensions (joy, sadness, anger, fear) to probe non-factual, semantic negation.

\section{Methodology}
\label{sec:method}
% =================================================================

\subsection{Direct Logit Attribution Analysis}
We treat the residual stream as a linear accumulator. At each layer, both MLP blocks and attention heads write vectors into this stream. Since the final logit for any token is the dot product of the final residual stream with the unembedding matrix, and the residual stream is a sum of all component outputs, we can directly attribute each component's scalar contribution to a target token's logit. We call this per-component score the Direct Logit Attribution (DLA). In our experiments, DLA is computed for the target factual token (e.g., " Paris") at the position of the final input token. FFN blocks in mid-to-late layers show strongly positive DLA values, meaning they push up the probability of the factual answer. Inhibition attention heads show negative DLA values, actively reducing that probability. A \textbf{negation failure} is formally defined as the event where the negated target token's vocabulary rank is higher (closer to rank 1) in the negated prompt than its rank in the positive prompt — i.e., the model is \emph{more} confident about the forbidden answer after seeing ``not''.

\subsection{The Signal-to-Gate Ratio (SGR)}
To quantify the competition between factual retrieval and logical suppression, we define the Signal-to-Gate Ratio (SGR) as:

\begin{equation}
\text{SGR} = \frac{\sum_{l} \max(\text{DLA}^{(l)}_{\text{FFN}}, 0)}{\sum_{l} |\min(\text{DLA}^{(l)}_{\text{Attn}}, 0)|}
\end{equation}

where the numerator sums all positive FFN DLA contributions (factual retrieval) and the denominator sums all negative attention DLA contributions (inhibition). An $\text{SGR} > 1$ means retrieval dominates suppression; the model is structurally set up to fail at negation. An $\text{SGR} \leq 1$ implies the inhibition capacity is sufficient to override retrieval.

\subsection{Inhibition Head Identification and Amplification}
Top inhibition heads are identified by ranking attention heads by their mean negative DLA on the negated prompt across a 1000-pair analysis set. We then test the causal role of these heads by amplifying their output vectors by a scalar factor $\lambda \in [1, 4]$ at inference time, measuring whether the negation failure rate decreases as $\lambda$ increases.

\subsection{Activation Patching}
Activation patching is applied at the dataset scale to localize the most causally powerful layers. For each prompt pair, we run a clean forward pass on the positive prompt and a corrupted forward pass on the negated prompt. We then patch residual stream activations, MLP outputs, or attention outputs from one layer at a time from the clean run into the corrupted run, measuring the resulting change in the target token's logit ($\Delta$logit). A large positive $\Delta$logit after patching indicates that the clean information at that layer is critical for correct negation handling.

\subsection{Soft and Emotion Negation}
For soft negation, we replace the `` not'' suffix with `` unlikely to be'', `` rarely'', and `` maybe'' and re-run the full SGR benchmark pipeline on\texttt{gpt2-small} and \texttt{pythia-160m}. For the emotion negation experiment, we construct balanced prompt pairs (e.g., ``I feel happy'' vs.~``I do not feel happy'') across four emotion categories (joy, sadness, anger, fear) and extract the final-token residual stream representation as an emotion direction vector. We then measure direction norms across layers, probe accuracy, distance-to-neutral and distance-to-opposite under negation, and the R$^2$ linearity of an alpha-sweep that injects the emotion direction vector back into the residual stream.

\section{Experimental Design}
\label{sec:pipeline}
% =================================================================

Our experiments run on \texttt{gpt2-small} (117M parameters, 12 layers, 12 heads) and \texttt{pythia-160m} (160M parameters, 12 layers, 12 heads) via the \texttt{TransformerLens} \citep{nanda2022} library. The core \textbf{cross-model quantitative analysis} processes the full CounterFact dataset (21,919 pairs for GPT-2 Small, 19,562 for Pythia-160M) and computes, for each pair: the target token's rank in both the positive and negated prompts; the per-layer DLA decomposition; the SGR; the Crossover Point (the first layer where cumulative attention DLA exceeds cumulative FFN DLA); and whether a negation failure occurred. A further 1000-pair subset is used for computationally intensive analyses (head-level DLA, amplification sweeps, activation patching). The \textbf{soft negation} experiment replaces the suffix on the same full dataset with four variants: `` not'', `` unlikely to be'', `` rarely'', and `` maybe''. The \textbf{emotion negation} experiment constructs a separate small dataset of 9-12 balanced affirmation/negation pairs per emotion category and runs direction-extraction, probe training, and alpha-sweep analysis on both models.

\section{Results}
\label{sec:results}
% =================================================================

\subsection{Cross-Model Quantitative Analysis}

\subsubsection{Negation Failure Rates and Rank Shifts}
Over the full CounterFact dataset, \texttt{gpt2-small} produced 2,498 negation failures out of 21,919 samples, a failure rate of \textbf{11.40\%}. \texttt{pythia-160m} showed 1,764 failures out of 19,562 samples, or \textbf{9.02\%}. On successful negations, both models achieve dramatic rank suppression: the median rank shift is $+668$ for GPT-2 Small and $+938.5$ for Pythia-160M, meaning the target token is pushed hundreds of positions deeper in the vocabulary distribution. On failures, the situation reverses sharply: the median rank shift is $-174.5$ for GPT-2 Small and $-296.5$ for Pythia-160M, confirming that the forbidden token actively moves \emph{up} the logit distribution when negation fails.

\subsubsection{SGR as a Predictor of Failure}
The SGR exhibits a clear bifurcation by outcome for GPT-2 Small. Successful negations have a median SGR of 24.83, while failed negations have a median SGR of \textbf{48.13} — nearly double. This aligns with the Competitive Gating Hypothesis: when FFN retrieval is unusually strong relative to inhibition capacity, the gate fails. Pythia-160M presents a different picture: the median SGR for successes (24.48) and failures (24.44) are nearly identical. This suggests that for Pythia, SGR as computed by an aggregate FFN/Attention ratio does not cleanly separate outcomes, implying a more distributed suppression mechanism in that model family.

\begin{figure}[h]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=\linewidth]{../../figures/cross_model/sgr_histogram.png}
    \caption{SGR distribution across CounterFact representations. Failures concentrate exclusively strictly to the right of SGR\,=\,1.}
    \label{fig:sgrhist}
\end{figure}

The SGR edge-case analysis reinforces this asymmetry: for GPT-2 Small, \textbf{0 failures occur at SGR $\leq$ 1} (failure mismatch rate = 0.00\%). However, 56.1\% of successes occur at SGR $> 1$ — confirming that high SGR is necessary but not sufficient for failure. For Pythia-160M, the mismatch is even more extreme: 93.97\% of successes occur at SGR $> 1$, indicating that Pythia overwhelmingly navigates negation even when the retrieval signal is quantitatively stronger than the measured inhibition.

\subsubsection{Top Inhibition Heads}
Heads were ranked by mean negative DLA over the 1,000-pair analysis subset. For \texttt{gpt2-small}, the top inhibition heads are located at layers 9, 10, 8, 0, and 11 (heads (9,8), (10,0), (8,11), (0,1), (11,2)). For \texttt{pythia-160m}, the top heads are (9,4), (8,1), (8,10), (7,8), (9,0). Notably, both models concentrate inhibition activity in layers 7--11, consistent with our prediction of a late-stage negation gate.

\subsubsection{Inhibition Head Amplification}
We amplified the output of the top 5 inhibition heads by a scale factor $\lambda \in [1.0, 4.0]$. At $\lambda = 4.0$, the failure rate on the 1,000-pair subset dropped from \textbf{10.60\%} to \textbf{5.50\%} for GPT-2 Small (absolute improvement: 5.1pp) and from \textbf{7.80\%} to \textbf{3.40\%} for Pythia-160M (absolute improvement: 4.4pp). This intervention provides causal evidence that the identified heads are directly responsible for a portion of negation failures.
\subsubsection{Activation Patching}
Dataset-scale activation patching reveals that residual-stream patching at a single layer produces the most effective rescue of negation behavior. For \texttt{gpt2-small}, fully patching the residual stream at \textbf{layer 9} produces the highest rank-shift delta of $+3.69$ logit units. MLP-level patching peaks at Layer 0 ($+2.90$) while attention-level patching peaks at Layer 2 ($+0.22$). For \texttt{pythia-160m}, the best layer is \textbf{layer 3} with a residual-stream delta of $+5.34$, MLP peaks at Layer 0 ($+2.68$) and attention at Layer 8 ($+0.23$). The large MLP effect at Layer 0 for both models suggests that very early layers establish a strong factual prior that dominates the residual stream from the beginning.

To ground the aggregate statistics, we analysed 10 shared cases across both models.

\textbf{Case 18316} (``The official religion of Urwah ibn Mas'ud is not [Islam]'') illustrates a severe, consistent failure. Both models fail: GPT-2 Small assigns the target its rank-1 position (rank drop $= -1$, SGR $= 65.9$); Pythia-160M jumps from rank 20 to rank 18 under negation (SGR $= 85.1$). The dominant layer-shift in GPT-2 Small is at layer 8 ($\Delta = -30.7$, top-3 layers explain 78.2\% of shift), indicating that a strong factual association implanted by MLP layers 5--8 overwhelms whatever inhibition is applied in layers 9--11.

\textbf{Case 17579} (``...the language spoken is not [German]'') shows the most extreme SGR in the entire dataset: \textbf{2896.2} for GPT-2 Small, with a rank that only moves from 5 (positive) to 3 (negated). The target probability actually increases under negation. Layer 11 alone contributes a positive DLA shift of $+34.6$, confirming the Pink Elephant hypothesis: late-layer MLP activity boosts the very concept the model was instructed to suppress.

\textbf{Case 13838} (``bpost, by not [Belgium]'') shows a model-asymmetry: GPT-2 Small successfully negates (rank $17{,}200 \to 40{,}250$, SGR $= 0.89$), while Pythia-160M completely fails (rank $17{,}786 \to 8{,}168$, SGR $= 20.0$). In Pythia, a Crossover Point is identified at layer 4, and MLP patching at layer 11 produces the best rescue ($\Delta = +21.96$), suggesting the suppression circuit exists but engages too late.

\textbf{Contrastive success case} (Case 2295, ``John Feaver is originally from not [Fleet]''): both models succeed strongly, pushing the target from rank 1,773 to 41,777 (GPT-2) and from rank 3,717 to 24,643 (Pythia). GPT-2 achieves this with SGR $= 0.0$ (zero measured inhibition), suggesting that for obscure entities, the FFN retrieval signal itself is so weak that no active inhibition is needed.

Our original hypothesis was that soft and probabilistic negators would fare \emph{worse} than the hard ``not'', as they theoretically provide a weaker logical signal. This hypothesis was \textbf{rejected}. Counter-intuitively, while hard negation is linguistically more explicit, both architectures exhibited significantly higher failure rates when processing it (14.19\% for GPT-2 Small; 9.39\% for Pythia-160M) compared to soft negators (dropping to $\sim$2-4\%). The suffix ``rarely'' yields the \emph{lowest} failure rate of any tested negator for both models.

Mechanistically, this improvement is reflected in the Signal-to-Gate Ratio (SGR). For GPT-2 Small, the median SGR drops from 27.09 for hard negation down to 9.67 for ``rarely'', indicating that soft negators trigger a geometrically stronger inhibitory attention response relative to FFN memory retrieval. We hypothesize this occurs due to two factors: computational depth and semantic magnitude. Multi-token negators like ``unlikely to be'' afford the attention circuit more forward-pass cycles to accumulate an inhibitory subtraction vector. Furthermore, semantically ``softer'' terms require less geometric distance to resolve than a strict polarity inversion, lowering the threshold of inhibitory force required to successfully suppress the hallucination.

\subsection{Emotion Negation Direction Analysis}

\subsubsection{Emotion Direction Geometry}
To understand how negation operates geometrically, we performed a comparative Principal Component Analysis (PCA) on the final-layer residual stream representations of polar emotion concepts. Analysis of the anchor coordinates reveals that the principal components naturally separate the semantic space: PC1 ($\sim$53\% explained variance) isolates emotional magnitude (separating Joy and Sadness from Neutrality), while PC2 ($\sim$8\% explained variance) isolates semantic polarity (separating Joy from Sadness). 

Tracking the layer-wise trajectory of negated prompts (e.g., ``I do not feel happy'') provides a direct geometric visualization of the Competitive Gating Hypothesis. In the early layers (L0--L6), both models exhibit a ``Pink Elephant'' effect: logic-blind Feed-Forward Networks perform factual retrieval, pulling the representation aggressively toward the positive ``Joy'' anchor along the PC1 axis, despite the explicit presence of a negator.

    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=\linewidth]{../../figures/emotion_negation/pca_negation_trajectory_comparison.png}
    \caption{Layer-wise PCA trajectories of negated emotion concepts. Both models exhibit logic-blind retrieval (moving toward Joy) before late-layer inhibition attempts vector subtraction. A massive architectural jump occurs at L11 as syntactic subspaces map to the output vocabulary matrix.}
    \label{fig:pca_negation}
\end{figure}

\subsubsection{Negation Geometry and the Excluded Middle}
At the Crossover Point (typically around L6 or L7), late-stage inhibitory attention heads activate to perform vector subtraction, pivoting the trajectory sharply away from the hallucinated concept. Notably, the trajectory from Layers 0 through 10 resides in a distant, orthogonal syntactic sub-space before executing a massive, discrete geometric jump into the target semantic space at Layer 11. This jump represents the exact architectural moment the models translate their internal math into the final pre-unembedding vocabulary matrix.

Critically, the models resolve this geometric tug-of-war differently in the final layer. Pythia-160M’s inhibition gate under-corrects; its final L11 state is dragged decisively past the neutral origin but falls short of perfectly aligning with the ``Sadness'' antonym, leaving the model stranded in a positive-skewed semantic compromise (an ``Excluded Middle''). Conversely, GPT-2 Small executes a dramatic over-correction, with its inhibitory heads pushing the residual stream entirely past the ``Sadness'' antonym on the polarity axis.

\subsubsection{Intervention Linearity}
To quantify the mechanism of this vector subtraction, we injected a scaled version of the emotion direction ($\alpha \cdot \hat{d}$) into the residual stream and measured the $R^2$ of a linear fit between $\alpha$ and the logit margin. 

In \texttt{gpt2-small}, Anger ($R^2 = 0.978$), Fear ($R^2 = 0.979$), and Sadness ($R^2 = 0.902$) are highly linear. Joy is a striking exception ($R^2 = 0.224$), indicating its representation is multi-modal and resists simple linear steering. In \texttt{pythia-160m}, Joy ($R^2 = 0.936$), Fear ($R^2 = 0.872$), and Sadness ($R^2 = 0.963$) are highly linear, while Anger is the outlier ($R^2 = 0.287$). Despite high $R^2$ values, intervention slopes are uniformly tiny (order $10^{-4}$). This indicates that while the inhibition gate operates via linear subtraction, emotional directions in these small models are often orthogonal to the principal decision-making subspace, explaining why the gate frequently struggles to fully overcome the magnitude of FFN retrieval.

\section{Analysis}
\label{sec:analysis}
% =================================================================

\subsection{When is Recall Logic-Blind?}
The per-layer DLA means confirm the core premise of the Competitive Gating Hypothesis. Early-to-mid FFN layers (approximately layers 3--8) inject strong, positive DLA contributions toward the factual target, with this pattern being \emph{nearly identical} between the positive and negated prompts. The FFN does not "see'' negation; it fires unconditionally based on the entity in context. The strongest inhibition from individual attention heads is a late phenomenon, concentrating in layers 7--11. However, a ``Crossover Point''---the first layer where \emph{cumulative} negative attention DLA exceeds cumulative positive FFN DLA---can typically be identified much earlier in the network, predominantly between layers 2 and 8. Instances where this crossover is delayed to a later layer, or completely fails to occur, are systematically associated with higher failure rates and larger SGR values.

\subsection{The SGR as a Bounded Predictor}
The SGR's predictive power is asymmetric. The iron rule that \emph{zero failures occur at SGR $\leq$ 1} holds for both models under hard ``not'' negation, validating the metric's lower bound. However, the majority of successes also have SGR $> 1$ (56\% for GPT-2 Small, 94\% for Pythia). This reveals a critical limitation: a high SGR is necessary but not sufficient for failure. Additional factors, such as the concentration of DLA in the final layers versus earlier layers and the sharpness of the crossover, further determine outcomes. The contrast between GPT-2 Small (where SGR does differentiate failures by median value) and Pythia-160M (where it does not) further suggests that Pythia has a more diffuse and robust inhibition circuit.

\subsection{Mechanistic Explanation of Soft Negation Success}
The most counterintuitive finding of our work is that multi-word soft negators (`` rarely'', `` unlikely to be'') \emph{outperform} explicit boolean negation. The mechanistic analysis from the SGR pipeline offers an explanation: for `` rarely'' in GPT-2 Small, the median SGR collapses from 27.09 (hard ``not'') to \textbf{9.67}. Given that:
\[
\text{SGR} = \frac{\text{FFN retrieval}}{\text{Attention inhibition}}

Rather than a reflex retrieval that overwhelms a gate, the failure here is one of \emph{geometric incompleteness}: models do not have a learned representation of ``not-emotion'' that coherently maps to the opposite pole. GPT-2 Small, with its high-norm late-layer emotion directions, performs crude semantic inversion (moving toward the opposite), but Pythia-160M frequently collapses into a neutral representation (78\% of ``not sad'' trials). This ``Excluded Middle'' fallacy shows that even when a model encodes emotion linearly (high $R^2$), the negation operator does not reliably reverse the direction in latent space. The `` maybe'' control acts as expected, producing the highest SGR and near-identical failure rates to hard negation, confirming that ambiguous hedges provide no inhibitory signal whatsoever.

\subsection{Emotion Negation and the Geometry of Suppression}
Our geometric analysis of the residual stream validates the Competitive Gating Hypothesis, revealing that logical negation is resolved through an active, layer-wise vector tug-of-war rather than a discrete binary switch. The emotion PCA trajectories prove that early-layer factual retrieval generates a massive momentum toward the negated entity. The subsequent architectural jump at Layer 11 demonstrates that models attempt to resolve this via late-stage inhibitory vector subtraction. However, the failure to perfectly align with the logical antonym—resulting in either Pythia's semantic compromise or GPT-2's geometric over-correction—shows that LLM negation is highly volatile. The final prediction is not a flawless logical operation, but rather the literal vector sum of a dominant factual memory signal colliding with an imperfect, model-specific inhibitory gate.

\section{Limitations and Challenges}
\label{sec:limitations}
% =================================================================
Our analysis isolates circuits exclusively using logit attribution and linear interventions in small models (up to 160M parameters). In larger open-source models, especially Mixture of Experts (MoE), identifying a single explicit "Crossover Point" becomes complicated by polysemantic routing. Furthermore, DLA struggles to capture highly non-linear head interactions, a problem that could be addressed in future work by using Sparse Autoencoders (SAEs) to unravel the dense superposition of the emotion vectors observed in our tests. A further practical limitation of our negation construction is that appending negators such as ``not'' or ``rarely'' directly to a prompt suffix is often syntactically ill-formed; this means the model is partially responding to a malformed sentence rather than a clean logical instruction, which may inflate or distort our measured failure rates. Finally, our core failure metric---the rank of the single target token---is an incomplete proxy: a negation may fail not because the model pushes the target token \emph{down}, but because it simultaneously promotes \emph{other} plausible tokens, and our single-token rank-change measure cannot distinguish these two mechanistically distinct failure modes.

\section{Conclusion}
\label{sec:conclusion}
% =================================================================
Negation in LLMs is not simply an abstract reasoning failure, but a physical capacity breakdown. Through this study, we tested two core hypotheses. First, the \textbf{Competitive Gating Hypothesis} was \textbf{validated}: dataset-scale DLA confirms that factual retrieval acts as a logic-blind FFN reflex, while negation acts as a structurally distinct attention-driven gate. Our Signal-to-Gate Ratio (SGR) proves that failures only occur when this early retrieval mathematically overwhelms downstream inhibition, a dynamic we causally verified via head amplification. Second, our \textbf{Soft Negation Hypothesis}---that linguistically softer negators would increase failure rates---was definitively \textbf{rejected}. Suffixes like ``rarely'' paradoxically reduced hallucinations and lowered the SGR compared to a hard ``not,'' revealing that multi-token or semantic-softness affords the network more cycles to compute a geometrically distinct inhibitory subtraction. By grounding the "Pink Elephant" effect within mechanistic circuits, this diagnostic approach represents a critical step toward re-engineering logical resilience in future models.
